Working Paper(s)

1. Sponsored Search
Job Market Paper

Abstract  Draft   Slides   Code  Data  BibTeX

 I develop a theory of digital marketplaces where an intermediary provides a platform for firms to list their product and where consumers engage in costly search to learn about the products. I find that when prices can be observed prior to costly search, a firm higher in the list charges a higher price and earns a larger profit. This result is in contrast with those from the standard models of prominence where prices are discovered after search. I augment this model by allowing the intermediary to determine, endogenously, the order in which products are displayed by conducting an auction. The effect of the auction on product prices is captured by the auction reserve price, which is chosen by the intermediary to maximise its commission from firms. I show that these commissions (paid per-click) enter the pricing equations as a fixed cost for the prominent firm while being analogous to marginal cost for the non-prominent firm. This asymmetry in equilibrium lowers consumer surplus, firms’ profits, conversion (ratio of buyers to visitors) rate of non-prominent firm and total transactions in the market.

Presented at: 2021 UPF Internal Micro, PhD Jamboree

Work In-Progress

1. Demand Distortions and markups with Sampreet Goraya

Abstract  Slides  

 Local demand is an important determinant of firm growth in the rural areas. In this project, we document the role of ethnic networks in segmenting the local demand and its implication for local firm growth and aggregate misallocation. We focus our analysis on Indian firms, where caste networks are an important determinant of individuals’ economic outcome. We show that demand is segmented along caste lines. This in turn creates markup and productivity dispersion. To identify this demand effect, we use exogenous changes in regional rainfall intensity as an instrument for local demand shocks. We find that the Low Caste (LC) consumers experience a positive income shock due to rainfall and that this translates into higher per-capita consumption. Further, we show that regional increase in the LCs’ demand translates into higher output growth for the firms with LC owners relative to others. Using a quantitative model of capital market friction and demand segmentation, we look to highlight the demand channel in determining firm growth and misallocation.

Presented at (me & co-author): 2020 PhD Jamboree
